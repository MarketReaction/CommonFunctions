Europe stocks wobble as ECB decision looms

By Sara Sjolin

Published: Sept 5, 2012 12:00 p.m. ET

Share

By

SaraSjolin

Markets reporter

LONDON (MarketWatch) — European stocks ended a choppy session withy marginal gains Wednesday, as investors awaited the outcome of a meeting the following day of European Central Bank policy makers.

The Stoxx Europe 600 index (SXXP) closed marginally higher at 265.49, after seesawing between gains and losses for most of the session.

“Economists don’t expect much at all from the ECB meeting [Thursday], and I think there will be no specific targets [on a bond-buying program]. But markets’ expectations are probably higher than that and they would like some specifications on duration and quantity,” said Jean-Baptiste Pethe, economist at Exane BNP Paribas.

“We expect the ECB to add strong words to mitigate the disappointment,” he added.

Among notable movers in Europe, shares of Nokia Corp. (NOK1V) NOK, -8.60% NOK, -8.60% plunged 13% after the handset maker unveiled a series of smartphones running Microsoft Corp.’s MSFT, -4.01% new Windows 8 software. See: Nokia lifts wraps on newest Lumia smartphone and See: Nokia placing a bet on Windows 8

Oil heavyweight BP PLC (BP.)  BP, -4.76% was also on the decline, its shares down 2.9%.

The Financial Times reported that the U.S. Justice Department will try to prove allegations of negligence or misconduct against the oil major, citing a memo filed in a New Orleans court. The charges are tied to the 2010 oil-spill disaster in the Gulf of Mexico. See: U.S. accusing BP of gross negligence in Gulf spill

A BP spokesperson said in an emailed statement it “believes it was not grossly negligent and looks forward to presenting evidence on this issue at trial in January.”

The loss drove London’s FTSE 100 index (UKX) 0.3% lower to 5,657.86. HSBC Holdings PLC (HSBA) HBC, -5.47% (5) also felt selling pressure, shedding 0.7%. Read more about U.K. stocks

All eyes on the ECB

For the broader stock markets in Europe, investors fixed their eyes on the European Central Bank’s policy decision due Thursday.

Some analysts are expecting an interest-rate cut to 0.5% from 0.75%, but the main question is whether ECB President Mario Draghi provides details of a proposed bond-buying program as a way to calm the region’s sovereign-debt crisis. See: Danger lurks if ECB disappoints Thursday

Alastair Ryan, analyst at UBS, said in a note that policy changes likely involve “some form of ECB intervention in certain sovereign-bond markets and an announcement of the intent to make the ECB lead regulator for some or all euro-area banks.”

“The former certainly can determine (low) bond yields for a period of time; the latter holds out the prospect of better bank regulation in [the] future. However, neither is a permanent fix for the euro’s travails; built-up expectations have the potential to be let down over the coming months,” he said.

Unlimited bond buying

Earlier Wednesday, a Bloomberg News report said the European Central Bank would consider launching an unlimited bond-buying program at the meeting, although without setting a public cap on yields. A program would, according to central bank officials, reportedly be sterilized to calm concerns about the total money supply, by removing the same amount of money elsewhere in the system.

On Tuesday, yields on Italian and Spanish government bonds plunged. Late Monday, according to media reports, Draghi had indicated in closed-door testimony before the European Parliament that a new bond-buying program could include bonds with maturities as long as three years without violating the central bank’s mandate.

Yields on benchmark 10-year Spanish government bonds (10YR_ESP) were down 19 basis point to 6.38% on Wednesday, according to electronic trading platform Tradeweb.

Yields on 10-year Italian paper (10YR_ITA) fell 16 basis points to 5.50%.

Pethe from Exane BNP Paribas said, however, that the European Central Bank should not only focus on bringing down sovereign yields but also turn to corporate borrowing costs.

“A credit-easing program buying corporate bonds could bring relief to southern economies and help the economy to grow,” he said. “Companies are financing at around 6%, which is quite high.”

Meanwhile, on the data front, the Markit euro-zone composite purchasing-managers’ index indicated private-sector activity in the region contracted more than previously estimated in August. See: Euro-zone August composite PMI falls to 46.3

And across the Atlantic, data showed that productivity for the U.S. nonfarm business sector rose 2.2% in second quarter, up from a 1.6% pace estimated a month ago. See: U.S. productivity revised higher in second quarter

U.S. stocks were higher on Wall Street.

Wednesday’s major movers

Shares of health care company Fresenius SE & Co. (FRE) rose 1.4% in Frankfurt after its Kabi unit raised its full-year outlook on the back of solid organic growth in the first half. See: Fresenius SE's Kabi unit raises 2012 outlook

The DAX 30 index (DAX) gained 0.5% to 6,964.69.

Among French stocks, STMicroelectronics NV (STM)(STM) dropped 4.9% after Exane BNP Paribas and UBS separately downgraded the chip maker, to ratings of underperform and sell respectively, both from neutral ratings previously.

The CAC 40 index (PX1), however, rose 0.2% to 3,405.79, with shares of BNP Paribas SA (BNP) up 2.1% and Société Générale SA (GLE) closing 2.5% higher.

(STM)Also higher, LeGrand SA (LR) rose 3%, after Barclays lifted its rating on the electrical installations firm to overweight from equal weight.
